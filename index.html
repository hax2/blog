Are there words LLMs won’t say?

When an LLM predicts the next token, it reads the context that came before and produces a logit for every token in its vocabulary.

Each possible next token gets a logit: a raw score before probabilities exist. Softmax uses the gaps between these scores. If popsicle gets logit 3 and cheese gets logit 1, the gap is 2, and that gap determines how strongly softmax favors popsicle.

For Llama 3, the vocabulary has 128k tokens. These include whole words, word pieces, punctuation, spaces, and other text fragments.

If the LLM always chooses the token with the highest score, this is called greedy decoding.

Temperature is an inference-time setting. It is usually treated as a creativity parameter: higher temperature makes the output more varied, while lower temperature makes the output more predictable. At T = 0, the model uses greedy decoding and selects the highest-scoring token.

For values above zero, temperature works by dividing the logits before softmax. Lower temperatures stretch the gaps between logits. Higher temperatures compress them.

Imagine the word popsicle gets the logit 3 and the word cheese gets the logit 1.

If we set the temperature to 2, each logit is divided by 2 before being converted into a probability: 

popsicle: 3 / 2 → 1.5
cheese:   1 / 2 → 0.5

The gap was originally:

3 - 1 = 2

After temperature scaling, the gap is:

1.5 - 0.5 = 1

Softmax turns logit gaps into probability ratios. A gap of 2 gives a ratio of e². A gap of 1 gives a ratio of e¹. When temperature shrinks the gap, the probabilities move closer together.

Temperature changes the gap before softmax

Move the temperature slider. The logits stay the same, but the scaled gap changes, and softmax gives the two tokens closer or more distant probabilities.

To turn the logits into probabilities, we use softmax. Softmax raises the mathematical constant e to the power of each logit, then divides that value by the sum of all the exponentiated logits.

In the example above, with temperature set to 2, the probabilities become: 

popsicle: 73%
cheese:   27%

With temperature set to 1, they become: 

popsicle: 88%
cheese:   12%

Increasing temperature flattens the probability distribution, giving lower-ranked tokens a better chance of being sampled. Decreasing temperature sharpens the distribution around the highest-scoring tokens.

A lower-probability token still has to survive the sampler.

Sampling filters reduce the set of possible tokens the model can choose from. Before filtering, every token has some probability, even if that probability is tiny. After filtering, many tokens are removed from the final choice.

The three common sampling filters are top-k, top-p, and min-p.

Top-k

For top-k, we choose a value of k, for example 5. The model ranks tokens by probability, keeps the first 5, and discards everything below rank 5. The final token is sampled from the surviving group.

Top-k keeps a fixed number of tokens

Change k. The cutoff moves by rank, regardless of the actual probability values.

Top-p

For top-p, also called nucleus sampling, we choose a probability threshold, for example 0.9. The model starts with the most likely token, then adds the next most likely token, then the next, until the kept tokens together account for at least 90% of the probability mass. Tokens beyond that cutoff are removed.

So if the top token has probability 40%, the kept set starts at 40%. If the second token has 20%, the kept set grows to 60%. If the third has 12%, it grows to 72%. The model keeps walking down the ranked list until the running total reaches the top-p threshold. After that, the surviving probabilities are renormalized so they add up to 100%.

Top-p keeps tokens until the running total reaches p

The dark segments survive. The grey segments are beyond the cutoff. The red line is the p threshold.

p = 90%

Min-p

For min-p, the cutoff is based on the probability of the most likely token. If min-p is 0.05 and the top token has probability 40%, then the cutoff is: 

0.05 × 40% = 2%

Every token below 2% is discarded. This makes min-p dynamic. When the top token is very strong, the cutoff becomes stricter. When the top token is weaker, the cutoff relaxes.

Min-p keeps tokens above a threshold relative to the top token

The red line is the cutoff. Tokens whose bars do not reach it are removed.

After temperature and sampling filters are applied, the model samples one token from the surviving set.

A token can have a real probability in the model’s distribution and still become impossible to output after filtering. Once the sampler removes it, the model cannot choose it in that step.

Word Coverage Score

The question behind Lost in Sampling is: are there ordinary human words that LLMs rarely or never get to use because they fail to survive the sampling process? And can we measure how much vocabulary gets cut off by normal decoding settings?

The paper introduces the Word Coverage Score, or WCS, to measure lexical reachability under sampling filters.

The question is mechanical.

Given a real human sentence where a target word actually appeared, and given the prefix before that word, would the target word survive the sampler at the exact moment the model would need to generate it?

If the answer is yes, the word is reachable.

If the answer is no, the word has been pruned. The model could not have generated that human word in that context under that sampler setting, no matter how many times we sampled, because the word was removed before the final choice.

There is one extra complication: words and tokens are different things.

A word like tyranny might be one token in one tokenizer and multiple tokens in another. For a word to count as reachable, every token needed to spell that word has to survive at every step. If the first token survives and the second token gets pruned, the word fails the reachability test.

WCS checks whether the whole word survives

Each row is a simplified word-context test. A word only counts as reachable if every token piece survives.

The protocol starts by choosing target words according to frequency in human writing. The paper focuses on words outside the top 10k but inside the top 40k. That avoids extremely common words while also avoiding words so rare that they become noise. This middle-long-tail band gives words like tyranny, circulate, and dubious.

Then each target word is placed back into real human-written contexts. The paper uses PG-19, a corpus of public-domain books, and tests 100 target words across 10 contexts each, for 1,000 word-context trials.

The model is then given the human-written prefix before the target word. At the exact point where the author used the target word, the audit checks whether the token or tokens needed for that word survive top-k, top-p, or min-p.

WCS ignores the final random choice and checks the candidate set. It asks whether the word was still available after sampling filters.

A high WCS means the target words often survived the sampler.

A low WCS means many of the target words were cut off.

A word-level WCS of zero means the word survived in none of its tested contexts under that model and sampler setting. In that experimental setup, the sampler has erased the word from the model’s usable vocabulary.

WCS is a binary reachability metric. It catches the hard version of lexical loss: cases where a word is removed outright. It does not capture softer cases where a word survives but receives such a low probability that it is almost never sampled. That makes WCS a conservative measure. The full loss of lexical variety may be larger than the score alone shows.

Sampling settings form a bottleneck between the vocabulary the model assigns probability to and the vocabulary the model can actually use.

A model can know the word tyranny.

It can assign tyranny some probability.

It can have tyranny in the right semantic neighborhood.

But if the sampler cuts it off, the word is gone from that generation path.

Decoding is the final gate between the model’s internal distribution and the text that appears on the screen.

The tradeoff is real. Looser sampling can preserve more words, but it can also let in more noise. Stricter sampling can improve local coherence, but it can also remove valid, contextually appropriate words.

Common decoding settings can make rare-but-plausible words unreachable.

That is what WCS measures: vocabulary access.

And if LLM-generated text becomes a larger share of what people read, write, imitate, and train future models on, this matters. A language model that repeatedly loses access to parts of the human lexicon will push generated text toward a narrower band of language.

The model may carry a wider vocabulary than it gets to use.

The sampler decides which words survive.
